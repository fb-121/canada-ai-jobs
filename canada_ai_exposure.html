Data sources & limitations

Occupations are drawn from the National Occupational Classification (NOC) 2021. Employment figures are sourced directly from Statistics Canada's 2021 Census and represent full-year, full-time workers who reported employment income in 2020. Part-time, seasonal and self-employed workers are not included in these counts, so figures are lower than total occupation headcount. Median wages are sourced from the ESDC Labour Market Information 2025 dataset (released November 2025). Hourly wages have been converted to annual equivalents using a standard 2,080-hour work year (40 hrs/week × 52 weeks).

Important caveats: All figures are national aggregates. Regional variation particularly between Alberta's resource economy and Ontario's finance and tech sectors is not captured. Employment figures reflect 2020 conditions captured in the 2021 Census this is the most recent comprehensive occupational employment data available in Canada at this level of detail. Note that 2020 was a COVID-affected reference year; occupations in healthcare, hospitality and food services may show atypical counts relative to long-run employment levels. Wages are sourced from ESDC 2025 and reflect current market rates. Using the best available source for each data type independently is standard practice in labour economics. There is a data vintage difference: employment reflects 2020 conditions while wages reflect 2025 market rates. This is an independent analysis; it is not affiliated with or endorsed by Statistics Canada or ESDC.

Scoring methodology

Each occupation is scored 0–10 on a single AI Exposure axis: how much will AI reshape this role over the next 5–10 years? The score combines two factors direct automation (AI performing the work) and indirect displacement (AI making workers so productive that fewer are needed).

The key signal is whether the job's core output is fundamentally digital. If a role can be performed entirely from a computer without physical presence, AI exposure is inherently high. Conversely, roles requiring manual dexterity, physical presence, real-time human judgment, or trusted interpersonal relationships have a natural structural barrier.

Scores were generated by prompting Claude (Anthropic) with each occupation's NOC title, typical duties and work environment, then applying a consistent rubric. Each score was reviewed and adjusted for cross-occupation consistency. For example, surgeons (3/10) score lower than medical transcriptionists (10/10) because while robotic surgery is advancing, surgeons retain physical precision, intraoperative judgment and legal accountability that transcriptionists do not. Medical transcriptionists have already been largely displaced by voice-to-text AI in practice.

View scoring prompt 
You are an expert analyst evaluating how exposed different occupations are to AI disruption in the Canadian labour market.

You will be given a NOC 2021 occupation title and its typical duties.

Rate the occupation's overall AI Exposure on a scale from 0 to 10.

AI Exposure measures: how much will AI reshape this occupation over the next 5-10 years? Consider both direct effects (AI automating tasks currently done by humans) and indirect effects (AI making each worker so productive that fewer are needed).

A key signal is whether the job's core output is fundamentally digital. If a role can be performed entirely from a computer without physical presence, AI exposure is inherently high. Conversely, roles requiring manual dexterity, physical presence, real-time human judgment, or trusted interpersonal relationships have a natural structural barrier.

Calibration anchors:
0-2: Minimal. Almost entirely physical or hands-on work in variable environments. AI has no meaningful impact on daily work. Examples: electrician, plumber, paramedic.
3-4: Low. Mostly physical or interpersonal work. AI may assist with peripheral tasks but does not touch the core job. Examples: registered nurse, physiotherapist, carpenter.
5-6: Moderate. Mix of physical and knowledge work. AI meaningfully assists with information-processing parts but substantial human presence still required. Examples: general practitioner, civil engineer, pharmacist.
7-8: High. Predominantly knowledge work. AI tools already useful and workers using AI may be substantially more productive. Core tasks increasingly automatable. Examples: lawyer, financial analyst, graphic designer.
9-10: Maximum. Routine information processing, fully digital, no physical component. AI can already perform most tasks today. Examples: bookkeeper, medical transcriptionist, data entry clerk.
